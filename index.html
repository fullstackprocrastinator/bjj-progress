01 — Core Philosophy

Move Yourself,
Not Them

When you're giving up 20+ pounds, you cannot play a game of physics and expect to win. If you meet force with force, the bigger person prevails. The answer is simple: stop trying to move them and start moving yourself.

Every technique in this playbook follows one rule — use their anchored mass as a reference point to redirect your own body. You become the weapon. They become the post.

The Pacing Advantage

Big grapplers carry extra muscle — it demands massive oxygen. Build your gas tank with HIIT, then use superior cardio to out-scramble bigger opponents. Survive the first three minutes. Attack when they fade.

Frames Over Muscle

A bigger opponent's goal is to flatten you. Always stay on a hip. Rely on frames — using the bones of your forearms and shins — to bear their weight. Elbows glued to ribs prevent isolation.

02 — Position

Taking the Back

The back is the great equalizer. It is the only position where their size offers zero offensive advantage. All their mass now works against them.

The Arm Drag

Secure a cross-grip on the wrist and a high grip on their triceps. Do not try to drag their weight — use their anchored arm as a post to scoot your own hips to the outside.

The key insight: you are moving around them, not moving them. Their defensive post becomes your lever.

Kimura Trap → Chair Sit

  1. Lock the Figure-FourWeave your arm through and secure a tight Kimura grip from top side control or half guard.
  2. Force the DefenseWhen they lock their hands or grab their belt, they've anchored their shoulder to your chest.
  3. Post and ClearStep your outside leg completely over their head, entering the "chair sit" position.
  4. Leverage the RollSit back toward their hips. Their own locked hands act as a lever, forcing the roll and exposing the back.
03 — Dominance

Maintaining the
Heavy Mount

Once you secure mount, the goal is to prevent the Upa (bridge). A bigger person can easily launch a smaller grappler if you play it wrong.

  • Play Low Mount First Drop your hips flat against theirs and insert grapevines (thread your feet under their ankles). Stretching their legs prevents them planting feet to bridge.
  • Spinal Misalignment Secure a deep crossface to turn their jaw away from you, and underhook the far arm. Where the head goes, the body follows — their bridge becomes a log roll you ride.
  • The Wet Blanket Do not squeeze tightly with your thighs — this makes you rigid and easy to tip. Relax your muscles, sink your weight, and make them carry 100% of your mass. Become water.
  • Transition Early If the mount feels unstable, transition to mount-triangle or arm-triangle early rather than fight for mount. Submissions from unstable positions are better than a good position reversed.
