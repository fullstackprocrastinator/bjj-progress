Survive and Dominate

A structural, mechanical approach to bypassing size and strength advantages on the mats.

Core Strategy: Moving Yourself

When you are giving up 20+ pounds, you cannot play a game of physics and expect to win. If you meet force with force, the bigger person prevails. Stop trying to move them and start moving yourself.

1. Frames Over Muscle

A bigger opponent's goal is to flatten you. Always stay on a hip. Rely on frames—using the bones of your forearms and shins—to bear their weight. Keep your elbows glued to your ribs to prevent isolation.

The Pacing Advantage

Big grapplers have to carry extra muscle, requiring massive amounts of oxygen. Use your Monday HIIT sessions to build the gas tank, then use that cardio engine to out-scramble bigger opponents during your Friday and Saturday rolls. Don't match their explosive bursts early—survive the first three minutes, then attack when they fade.

Taking the Back

The back is the great equalizer. It is the only position where their size offers zero offensive advantage.

The Arm Drag

Secure a cross-grip on the wrist and a high grip on their triceps. Do not try to drag their weight; use their anchored arm as a post to scoot your own hips to the outside.

The Kimura Trap to Chair Sit

  1. Lock the Figure-Four: Weave your arm through and secure a tight Kimura grip from top side control or half guard.
  2. Force the Defense: When they lock their hands or grab their belt, they have anchored their shoulder to your chest.
  3. Post and Clear: Step your outside leg completely over their head, entering a "chair sit" position.
  4. Leverage the Roll: Sit back toward their hips, pulling the grip with you. Their own locked hands will act as a lever, forcing them to roll and exposing their back for your hooks.

Maintaining the Heavy Mount

If you secure the mount, do not let them launch you back to guard with an Upa (bridge).

  • Play Low Mount First: Drop your hips flat against theirs and insert grapevines (threading your feet under their ankles). Stretch their legs out so they cannot plant their feet to bridge.
  • Spinal Misalignment: Secure a deep crossface to turn their jaw away from you, and underhook the far arm. Where the head goes, the body follows.
  • The Wet Blanket: Do not squeeze tightly with your thighs—this makes you rigid and easy to tip over. Relax your muscles, sink your weight, and make them carry 100% of your mass.

The Guard Gallery

Guards that dictate distance and keep their weight off your center line.

[ Image: Knee Shield / Z-Guard ]

Knee Shield

Place your top shin across their chest or biceps to create a structural wall. Perfect for stopping the smash pass.

[ Image: Butterfly Guard ]

Butterfly Guard

Uses your feet as hooks inside their thighs. Fall back and use their own forward driving momentum to elevate and sweep them.

[ Image: Spider Guard ]

Spider Guard

Control the sleeves and place your feet directly on their biceps. This pits the strength of your leg press against their shoulders.
